\title{Riemannian versus KL Discretization of the Gaussian\\
Natural Gradient Flow: Convergence, Cost, and Stepsize Stability}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The Gaussian natural gradient flow for variational inference,
$\dot m=Cg$, $\dot C=C+CHC$, can be time-discretized in (at least) two ways that
share the same explicit mean update but differ in the covariance update: a
\emph{Riemannian-distance} (geodesic / exponential-map) step and a
\emph{KL/Bregman} (rational inverse-covariance) step. The current analysis of the
KL scheme carries a sufficient stepsize condition that is markedly smaller than
that of the Riemannian scheme, by a factor
$\max\{1,\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)\}$ that can be enormous for
ill-conditioned covariances. We test whether this smaller KL stepsize bound is a
genuine restriction or an artifact of the proof. On three deterministic
two-dimensional targets---an exact Gaussian posterior, a non-separable quartic
log-concave posterior, and a smooth strongly log-concave posterior---and a scalar
diagnostic, we classify every $(\text{target},\lambda,\text{method},\Delta t)$
run as SPD-feasible, stable, monotone, or accurate, and compare the empirical
maximum stepsizes against the theoretical bounds. The empirical KL stability
limit exceeds its theoretical bound by four to six orders of magnitude, far more
than the Riemannian scheme's one-to-two orders, indicating the KL stepsize
condition is conservative. A scalar diagnostic isolates the cause: the KL
covariance update is unconditionally well posed for log-concave targets, and the
large-stepsize failures of \emph{both} schemes are driven by the shared explicit
mean update, not by the covariance step. Beyond stability, we conduct a
comprehensive convergence-speed study across all three targets, three
conditionings $\lambda$, and every convergent stepsize (Sec.~\ref{app:speed}):
at fine stepsizes the two schemes converge at essentially the same rate in
elapsed time $n\Delta t$ and the iteration count to a fixed tolerance scales as $1/\Delta t$,
while at the coarsest convergent stepsizes they separate: on the two globally
smooth targets the Riemannian (geodesic) update converges faster per step than KL,
retaining higher fidelity to the continuous flow, whereas on the non-smooth
quartic target KL is the faster scheme. Finally, on a common Riemannian-scale
reference stepsize $\Delta t_{\mathrm{ref}}=1/(\beta\lambda_{\max})$ we benchmark
the theorem-predicted contraction factors of both schemes against the observed
contraction (Sec.~\ref{sec:rate}): the observed contraction is consistently
faster than either formula predicts, and the KL formula is the more conservative
of the two even at matched Riemannian-scale stepsizes, reinforcing that the KL
disadvantage lies in the current proof rather than in the scheme.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

We study the Gaussian variational inference problem
$\min_{m,C}\mathcal{E}(a)$ with $\mathcal{E}(a)=\KL(\rho_a\,\|\,\rho_\post)$, $\rho_a=\N(m,C)$,
$a=(m,C)$, $C\in\SPD(d)$, and the natural gradient flow that minimizes it in the
Fisher--Rao geometry. Writing
\begin{equation}
g_n=\E_{\rho_{a_n}}\!\big[\grad_\theta\log\rho_\post(\theta)\big],
\qquad
H_n=\E_{\rho_{a_n}}\!\big[\grad^2_\theta\log\rho_\post(\theta)\big],
\end{equation}
the continuous flow is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:flow}
\dot m_t = C_t g_t,
\qquad
\dot C_t = C_t + C_t H_t C_t .
\end{equation}
Two natural discretizations of \eqref{eq:flow} share the same explicit mean
update and differ only in how the covariance is advanced. The Riemannian-distance
discretization takes a true geodesic step of the affine-invariant geometry; the
KL/Bregman discretization takes a proximal/mirror step in the dually flat
(Bregman) geometry of the exponential family. The two are equally faithful to the
continuous flow to first order, but their finite-stepsize behavior, and in
particular the sufficient stepsize conditions under which the current analysis
proves descent, differ substantially.

The central question is sharp. The Riemannian proof admits stepsizes up to
$\Delta t_{\mathrm{Riem,theory}}=1/(\beta\lambda_{\max})$, whereas the KL proof
admits only
$\Delta t_{\mathrm{KL,theory}}=1/\big(\beta\lambda_{\max}\max\{1,\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)\}\big)$.
The extra factor is $1$ only when the relevant covariance eigenvalues are
comparable; for the ill-conditioned states that arise from a poorly scaled
initialization it is astronomically large, and the KL bound becomes vanishingly
small. We ask whether the KL scheme actually \emph{needs} such tiny stepsizes, or
whether the restriction is an artifact of the bounding argument---mirroring the
dimension-artifact question studied for the local rate in the companion report.

All experiments here are one- or two-dimensional, fully deterministic (closed-form
or Gauss--Hermite expectations, no Monte Carlo), and CPU-only, so that the
stepsize and stability conclusions are not confounded by sampling error.

\section{Algorithms}\label{sec:algos}

Both schemes evaluate $g_n$ and $H_n$ once at the current state $a_n=(m_n,C_n)$
and share the explicit mean update
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mean}
m_{n+1}=m_n+\Delta t\,C_n g_n .
\end{equation}
They differ only in the covariance update.

\paragraph{Riemannian-distance discretization.}
A geodesic step of the affine-invariant geometry,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:riem}
C_{n+1}=e^{\Delta t}\,C_n^{1/2}\,
\exp\!\big(\Delta t\,C_n^{1/2}H_nC_n^{1/2}\big)\,C_n^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
This is geometrically faithful---it is exactly the exponential map of the
metric along the natural-gradient direction---and it preserves positive
definiteness automatically, since it conjugates a matrix exponential (always SPD)
by $C_n^{1/2}$. Its cost is a symmetric eigendecomposition (for $C_n^{1/2}$ and
the matrix exponential) per step; in the $d\le2$ regime studied here this is
negligible, but in general it is the more expensive update.

\paragraph{KL/Bregman discretization.}
A proximal step in the dually flat geometry of the Gaussian exponential family,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl}
C_{n+1}=(1+\Delta t)\big(C_n^{-1}-\Delta t\,H_n\big)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
This is computationally simpler---a single symmetric solve of the precision
matrix $P_n=C_n^{-1}-\Delta t\,H_n$---and exposes a structural fact we will use
repeatedly: when the target is log-concave, $H_n=-\E[\grad^2 V]\preceq0$, so
$P_n=C_n^{-1}-\Delta t\,H_n\succ0$ for \emph{every} $\Delta t>0$ and the KL
covariance update is unconditionally well posed and SPD. Any KL failure must
therefore come from elsewhere in the iteration.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
Both covariance updates symmetrize the result, $C\leftarrow(C+C^\top)/2$, after
every step. The Riemannian step uses the eigendecomposition of the symmetric
argument for $C^{1/2}$ and $\exp$; the KL step forms $C_n^{-1}$ by a Cholesky
solve, builds $P_n$, and inverts it through its Cholesky factor. We record the
extreme eigenvalues of $C_n$ at every step and treat any NaN/Inf, loss of
positive definiteness ($\lambda_{\min}(C_n)\le10^{-12}$), or energy explosion as
a failure.

\paragraph{Consistency.}
Linearizing \eqref{eq:riem} and \eqref{eq:kl} in $\Delta t$ gives, for both,
$C_{n+1}=C_n+\Delta t\,(C_n+C_nH_nC_n)+O(\Delta t^2)$, matching $\dot C$ in
\eqref{eq:flow}; together with \eqref{eq:mean} both schemes are first-order
consistent with the continuous flow. (The test suite verifies this numerically by
finite differences.)

\section{Targets}\label{sec:targets}

We use three two-dimensional targets and one scalar diagnostic. Throughout,
$V=-\log\rho_\post$ up to a constant, $g=-\E[\grad V]$, $H=-\E[\grad^2 V]$, and
the energy minimized (the KL up to an $a$-independent constant) is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F}
\mathcal{E}(a)=\mathcal{E}(m,C)=-\tfrac12\log\det C+\E_{\N(m,C)}[V(\theta)],
\end{equation}
whose gap $\mathcal{E}(a_n)-\mathcal{E}(a_\star)$ is the convergence diagnostic
plotted throughout.
The two-dimensional targets all use $\lambda\in\{0.01,0.1,1\}$ and the
ill-conditioning is intentional: the proof factor
$\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)$ is largest exactly when the initial
covariance and the target curvature span a wide range of scales.

\paragraph{Gaussian posterior (target A).}
$\Phi_R(\theta)=\tfrac12\theta^\top Q\theta$ with $Q=\mathrm{diag}(1,\lambda)$, so
$\rho_\post=\N(0,Q^{-1})$ and
\begin{equation}
g_n=-Qm_n,\qquad H_n=-Q
\end{equation}
are exact and state-independent. The optimum is exact, $m_\star=0$,
$C_\star=Q^{-1}$, and the energy gap is analytic,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gauss-gap}
\mathcal{E}(a_n)-\mathcal{E}(a_\star)=\tfrac12\big[m_n^\top Qm_n+\Tr(QC_n)-\log\det(QC_n)-2\big].
\end{equation}
Initialization $m_0=(10,10)^\top$, $C_0=\mathrm{diag}(\tfrac12,2)$. With
$\grad^2 V\equiv Q$ the global constants are $\alpha=\min(1,\lambda)$,
$\beta=\max(1,\lambda)$. This is the cleanest stepsize test: there is no
quadrature or sampling error anywhere.

\paragraph{Non-smooth (quartic) log-concave posterior (target B).}
$\Phi_R(x,y)=\tfrac{(\sqrt\lambda x-y)^2}{20}+\tfrac{y^4}{20}$. This is convex but
\emph{not} globally smooth: $\grad^2 V$ grows without bound through the quartic
term, so no theorem-based global $(\alpha,\beta)$ exists and we set
\texttt{theory\_bound\_available=false}; only empirical stepsize thresholds are
reported. We refer to this target as \emph{non-smooth log-concave} throughout, the
defining property being the absence of a global smoothness constant. All required
expectations close in the Gaussian moments of $Y$,
$\E[Y^2]=m_y^2+C_{22}$, $\E[Y^3]=m_y^3+3m_yC_{22}$,
$\E[Y^4]=m_y^4+6m_y^2C_{22}+3C_{22}^2$, so $g$, $H$ and $\E[V]$ are exact.
Initialization $m_0=(10,10)^\top$, $C_0=4I$. The optimum $a_\star$ is found
numerically (below).

\paragraph{Smooth strongly log-concave posterior (target C).}
$\Phi_R^{\mathrm{smooth}}(x,y)=\tfrac{(\sqrt\lambda x-y)^2}{20}
+\tfrac{\delta}{2}(x^2+y^2)+\gamma\log\cosh(y)$ with $\delta=0.05$, $\gamma=1$.
This shares the non-separable coupled term of target B but is globally strongly
log-concave and smooth, with
\begin{equation}
\alpha=\delta,\qquad \beta=\delta+\tfrac{1+\lambda}{10}+\gamma,
\end{equation}
from the rank-one coupled quadratic (top eigenvalue $(1+\lambda)/10$), the
$\delta I$ ridge, and $0\le\gamma\,\mathrm{sech}^2(y)\le\gamma$. Expectations of
$\tanh(Y)$, $\mathrm{sech}^2(Y)$ and $\log\cosh(Y)$ under $Y\sim\N(m_y,C_{22})$
are computed by $80$-node Gauss--Hermite quadrature (deterministic, no Monte
Carlo). Initialization $m_0=(10,10)^\top$, $C_0=4I$.

\paragraph{Scalar Gaussian diagnostic (target D).}
$\rho_\post=\N(0,1)$, so $g=-m$, $H=-1$, and the covariance recursions reduce to
the closed forms
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scalar}
\text{KL: }C_{n+1}=\frac{(1+\Delta t)C_n}{1+\Delta t\,C_n},
\qquad
\text{Riem: }C_{n+1}=C_n\exp\!\big(\Delta t(1-C_n)\big),
\end{equation}
with the shared mean step $m_{n+1}=(1-\Delta t\,C_n)m_n$. The KL form gives
$C_{n+1}-1=(C_n-1)/(1+\Delta t\,C_n)$, a strict contraction toward $C=1$ for any
$\Delta t>0$. We sweep $C_0\in\{0.01,0.1,10,100\}$ and
$\Delta t\in\{0.5,1,2,5\}$, plus a mean-overshoot probe $m_0=1$, $C_0=100$.

\paragraph{Reference optimum.}
For the non-Gaussian targets B and C we minimize \eqref{eq:F} over the Cholesky
parameterization $C=LL^\top$, $L_{11}=e^{s_1}$, $L_{22}=e^{s_2}$, $L_{21}$ free,
with L-BFGS-B and analytic gradients (via the Bonnet/Price identities
$\partial_m\E[V]=\E[\grad V]$, $\partial_C\E[V]=\tfrac12\E[\grad^2 V]$) from several
deterministic restarts. The fitted optima are stationary to
$\|\,C_\star^{-1}+H_\star\|_F<10^{-9}$; optimizer diagnostics are saved to
\texttt{target\_metadata.json}.

\paragraph{Continuous-time reference.}
For each target/$\lambda$ we also integrate \eqref{eq:flow} from the same initial
condition with \texttt{solve\_ivp} (Radau, $\mathrm{rtol}=10^{-10}$,
$\mathrm{atol}=10^{-12}$), flattening the symmetric $C$ state and symmetrizing the
vector field. This continuous reference is used only for the discretization
\emph{accuracy} metric and the overlaid reference curves; the stability
conclusions do not depend on it.

\section{Stepsize criteria}\label{sec:criteria}

We sweep
$\Delta t\in\{0.001,0.002,0.005,0.01,0.02,0.05,0.1,0.2,0.5,1,2,5,10\}$
to a fixed terminal time $T=15$, taking $N=\lceil T/\Delta t\rceil$ steps per run
so that $N\Delta t=T$ (up to the final partial step): every run integrates to the
same elapsed time $T$, and the elapsed time after step $n$ is $n\Delta t$. The
coarsest stepsizes ($\Delta t=5,10$) are deliberate very-coarse
stability probes. Each run is classified from its \emph{full} trajectory (the
long-format CSV may be decimated for size, but classification always uses every
step).

\begin{definition}[Feasibility classes]\label{def:classes}
With $\mathcal{E}_n=\mathcal{E}(a_n)$ and $\mathcal{E}_\star=\mathcal{E}(a_\star)$, a run is
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{SPD-feasible} if it has no NaN/Inf and $\lambda_{\min}(C_n)>10^{-12}$
  for all $n$;
  \item \textbf{stable} if SPD-feasible, $\mathcal{E}_N<\mathcal{E}_0$, and there is no explosion,
  $\mathcal{E}_n-\mathcal{E}_\star\le10^3(\mathcal{E}_0-\mathcal{E}_\star)$ for all $n$;
  \item \textbf{monotone} if SPD-feasible and $\mathcal{E}_{n+1}\le \mathcal{E}_n+10^{-10}$ for all $n$;
  \item \textbf{accurate} if SPD-feasible and the terminal discretization error
  against the continuous reference satisfies
  $\|m_N-m(T)\|_2+\|C_N-C(T)\|_F/\|C(T)\|_F\le10^{-2}$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}
For each $(\text{target},\lambda,\text{method})$ we report the largest feasible
stepsize in each class,
$\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{SPD}}$, $\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{stable}}$,
$\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{monotone}}$, $\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{accurate}}$
(a plain maximum over passing stepsizes, so a non-monotone feasibility boundary
is captured faithfully).

\begin{definition}[Theoretical stepsizes]\label{def:theory}
For the globally smooth targets (A, C), set
$\lambda_{\min}=\min\{\lambda_{0,\min},1/\beta\}$ and
$\lambda_{\max}=\max\{\lambda_{0,\max},1/\alpha\}$ from the extreme eigenvalues of
$C_0$. The proof Lipschitz constants and sufficient stepsizes are
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{Riem,theory}}=\frac1{\beta\lambda_{\max}},
\qquad
\Delta t_{\mathrm{KL,theory}}=\frac1{\beta\lambda_{\max}\,
\max\{1,\ \lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)\}} .
\end{equation}
For the non-smooth target B (no global smoothness constant) no such bound is reported.
\end{definition}
The diagnostic quantities are the ratios
$\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{stable}}/\Delta t_{\mathrm{theory}}$ and
$\Delta t_{\max}^{\mathrm{monotone}}/\Delta t_{\mathrm{theory}}$: a ratio close to
$1$ means the proof bound is tight; a ratio of many orders of magnitude means the
bound is conservative. Because the KL theoretical stepsize carries the extra
$\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)$ factor, its ratio is the focus of the
proof-artifact discussion.

\paragraph{Convergence.}
Figures~\ref{fig:gap-gauss}--\ref{fig:gap-smooth} show the energy gap
$\mathcal{E}(a_n)-\mathcal{E}(a_\star)$ against elapsed time $n\Delta t$ for
representative stepsizes
$\Delta t\in\{0.1,0.5,1\}$. On the two globally smooth targets both schemes
converge cleanly at these stepsizes; the curves of the two methods are
visually close at matched $\Delta t$, since they agree to first order and the
shared mean update dominates the transient. The Gaussian target reaches the
analytic optimum to machine precision; the smooth target converges to the
numerically optimized $a_\star$. On the non-smooth target, both schemes require much
smaller stepsizes (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap-lit}), consistent with its unbounded
curvature: the representative stepsizes shown there are the largest that
converge for that target ($\Delta t\in\{0.005,0.01,0.02\}$), an order of
magnitude below those of the smooth targets.

\paragraph{Stepsize stability.}
Figures~\ref{fig:heat-gauss}--\ref{fig:heat-lit} are the stability heatmaps:
for each $(\lambda,\Delta t)$ a cell is green (stable and monotone), amber (stable
but non-monotone), or red (unstable). For the two smooth targets the dotted line
marks the theoretical stepsize $\Delta t_{\mathrm{theory}}$ for that method. Two
features are immediate. First, for the KL scheme the green/amber region extends
\emph{orders of magnitude} to the right of the dotted theoretical line---the
empirical stability boundary sits near $\Delta t\approx1$--$2$ while the proof
admits only $\Delta t\approx10^{-5}$--$10^{-6}$. Second, the Riemannian theoretical
line sits much closer to its empirical boundary. The same picture holds across all
three $\lambda$ and both smooth targets.

\paragraph{Theory vs empirical (the proof-artifact figure).}
Figure~\ref{fig:theory-vs-emp} and Table~\ref{tab:disc-step} compare, for the two
smooth targets, the theoretical stepsize against the empirical stable, monotone,
and accurate maxima. We emphasize the \emph{monotone} and \emph{accurate} classes
over bare stability: monotonicity certifies genuine descent at every step, and
accuracy certifies that the discrete trajectory still tracks the continuous flow
to $10^{-2}$, so both are stronger evidence than non-divergence alone. The
empirical KL monotone stepsize exceeds its theoretical bound by ratios from
$6.4\times10^{1}$ (Gaussian, $\lambda=1$) up to $8\times10^{8}$ (Gaussian,
$\lambda=0.01$), with the smooth target consistently around $1.4$--$2.0\times10^{5}$;
the \emph{accurate} maximum is one to two grid steps smaller but still exceeds the
KL bound by four to eight orders of magnitude, so the proof-artifact gap is not an
artifact of the permissive stability criterion---it persists under the strict
accuracy criterion. The Riemannian ratios, by contrast, range only from $2$ to
$200$ (monotone) and are of the same order under accuracy. The KL bound is
thus conservative by four to six extra orders of magnitude relative to the
Riemannian bound---precisely the size of the
$\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)$ factor that separates the two theoretical
stepsizes.

\paragraph{Scalar diagnostic.}
Figure~\ref{fig:scalar} traces the scalar covariance recursions \eqref{eq:scalar}
from $C_0\in\{0.01,0.1,10,100\}$ at $\Delta t\in\{0.5,1,2,5\}$. The KL covariance
update contracts to $C=1$ for every $C_0$ and every stepsize shown---as the closed
form $C_{n+1}-1=(C_n-1)/(1+\Delta t\,C_n)$ guarantees---never leaving the SPD cone.
The Riemannian scalar update $C_{n+1}=C_n\exp(\Delta t(1-C_n))$ can overshoot for
large $C_0\Delta t$. The mean-overshoot probe ($m_0=1$, $C_0=100$) confirms the
companion fact: the shared explicit mean step $m_{n+1}=(1-\Delta t\,C_n)m_n$
amplifies the mean for both schemes once $\Delta t\,C_n>2$ (e.g.\ $|m|$ grows past
$10^4$ at $\Delta t=5$ even though the KL covariance remains perfectly stable).
This locates the large-stepsize instability in the mean update shared by both
schemes, not in the KL covariance step.

\paragraph{Cost.}
Since the dimension is $d\le2$, wall-clock differences between the schemes are
small and noisy and we do not draw strong cost conclusions from them.
Figure~\ref{fig:cost} reports iterations-to-tolerance (gap $\le10^{-4}$) for stable
runs as the meaningful efficiency axis: at matched $\Delta t$ the two schemes need
a comparable iteration count, and the practical advantage of a scheme is governed
by how large a stable $\Delta t$ it admits rather than by per-step cost. The KL
covariance step (one symmetric solve) is the cheaper update per step; the
Riemannian step (eigendecomposition for $C^{1/2}$ and $\exp$) is more expensive in
higher dimension, though that difference is immaterial here.

\section{Convergence speed: Riemannian versus KL at matched stepsize}\label{app:speed}

The preceding sections concern \emph{stability}: how large a stepsize each
scheme tolerates and whether the KL proof bound is conservative. We now address
the complementary and practically decisive question of \emph{convergence speed}:
given a stepsize that converges for both schemes, which scheme reaches the
optimum faster, and how does that depend on the target, the conditioning
$\lambda$, and the stepsize? This is the question a practitioner choosing between
the two updates actually faces. Throughout we restrict to the convergent (stable)
stepsizes for each $(\text{target},\lambda,\text{method})$; divergent stepsizes
are excluded by construction, since a diverging run has no meaningful convergence
speed.

\paragraph{Summary: Riemannian usually wins at a matched stepsize.}
Table~\ref{tab:disc-conv} summarizes the head-to-head comparison. For each target
and stepsize it reports the terminal energy gap of each scheme (geometric mean
over the three $\lambda$), the faster scheme, and the factor separating them. The
pattern is clear and is the main practical message of this section. On the two
\emph{globally smooth} targets the Riemannian scheme wins at every stepsize, and
its advantage \emph{grows with the stepsize}: from a modest $1.3$--$7\times$ at the
finest stepsizes to $10^{4}$--$10^{8}\times$ smaller terminal gap at the coarsest
convergent stepsizes. Only on the non-smooth quartic target---whose unbounded
curvature drives the Riemannian exponential map near its stability edge at the
coarsest stable stepsize---does KL win, and there only by a modest factor. So in
the regime where one routinely operates (a globally smooth target, a stepsize
chosen as large as stability allows), the geodesic Riemannian update is the
faster of the two, often by many orders of magnitude.

\paragraph{Energy gap at matched stepsizes.}
Figures~\ref{fig:speed-gauss}--\ref{fig:speed-lit} show the comparison
panel by panel: one figure per target, with rows indexing the conditioning
$\lambda$ and columns indexing a representative convergent stepsize, so that
each panel contains exactly two curves---Riemannian (solid) and KL
(dashed)---at one $(\lambda,\Delta t)$ cell. The stepsizes shown are the curated
convergent set for each target (stable for both schemes at every $\lambda$);
the non-smooth target uses stepsizes an order of magnitude smaller, reflecting its
unbounded curvature. On the two \emph{globally smooth} targets
(Figs.~\ref{fig:speed-gauss}--\ref{fig:speed-smooth}) the comparison splits
cleanly by stepsize regime. At the
\emph{fine} stepsizes (left columns) the Riemannian and KL curves are nearly
indistinguishable: the two schemes agree to first order, so when $\Delta t$ is
small they descend along essentially the same gap-versus-$n\Delta t$ curve, and
that curve is the same across columns---the discrete trajectories track the
common continuous flow. At the \emph{coarse} stepsizes (right columns), however,
the schemes separate visibly: the Riemannian update converges markedly faster
per unit elapsed time $n\Delta t$, reaching machine precision while KL plateaus
several
orders of magnitude higher (at the Gaussian target, $\lambda=0.01$, $\Delta t=1$,
the terminal gaps are $\sim\!10^{-14}$ for Riemannian against $\sim\!10^{-7}$ for
KL). The geodesic covariance update therefore retains higher-order fidelity to
the flow as the stepsize coarsens, whereas the rational KL update accumulates a
larger discretization error---a genuine per-step convergence-rate advantage for
the Riemannian scheme at large stepsizes, distinct from the stability question of
the preceding sections. The non-smooth quartic target
(Fig.~\ref{fig:speed-lit}) is the exception: there the two schemes agree at the
finer stepsizes but \emph{KL} is the faster scheme, decisively so at the coarsest
convergent $\Delta t=0.02$, where the Riemannian exponential map sits near its
stability edge while the rational KL update has already converged. In all cases
both schemes remain monotone and convergent throughout; the
difference is the rate, not whether they converge.

\paragraph{The cost of a stepsize.}
Figure~\ref{fig:speed-cost} quantifies the efficiency consequence. For the stable
runs we measure the iterations and the elapsed time $n\Delta t$ required to reach a fixed
energy-gap tolerance ($10^{-6}$), averaged over $\lambda$, as a function of
$\Delta t$. The iteration count (top row) falls as $1/\Delta t$ on the log--log
axes---a larger convergent stepsize reaches the same tolerance in proportionally
fewer steps---while the elapsed time $n\Delta t$ to that tolerance (bottom row) is nearly
flat in $\Delta t$ until the largest convergent stepsizes, where the discrete
trajectory begins to lag the continuous flow. Consistent with the matched-stepsize
panels, the two schemes coincide at the fine and intermediate stepsizes and
separate only at the coarsest convergent stepsizes, where the faster scheme
reaches the tolerance in fewer iterations (and the slower one may not reach
$10^{-6}$ at all within the horizon, terminating at a larger plateau). Which
scheme that is depends on the target: on the two smooth targets it is Riemannian,
while on the non-smooth target it is KL---at $\Delta t=0.02$ the Riemannian run never reaches
$10^{-6}$ whereas KL does. The practical reading
is twofold: the dominant efficiency lever remains how large a stable stepsize a
scheme admits (the conclusion of the stability sections), but among the stepsizes both
schemes can take, the geometrically faithful Riemannian update converges faster
per step in the coarse-stepsize regime on smooth targets, while the two are
interchangeable when $\Delta t$ is small.

\section{Theoretical rate benchmark at Riemannian-scale stepsizes}\label{sec:rate}

The stability sections established that the KL proof stepsize is conservative,
and the convergence-speed section compared the two schemes empirically at matched
stepsizes. We now test a complementary question: do the theorem-predicted
\emph{contraction factors} themselves predict the observed contraction, and how
much slack do they carry at practically meaningful stepsizes? This isolates the
quality of the rate bounds from the question of the maximal admissible stepsize.

\paragraph{Why only the two globally smooth targets.}
We restrict to the exact Gaussian posterior (target A) and the smooth strongly
log-concave posterior (target C), because both satisfy global $\alpha$-strong
log-concavity and $\beta$-smoothness, so a clean theorem contraction factor
exists. We exclude the non-smooth quartic target B: it has no global $\beta$, so
no theorem rate is available to benchmark against.

\paragraph{A common Riemannian-scale reference stepsize.}
We deliberately use the \emph{Riemannian-scale} reference stepsize
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dtref}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{ref}}=\frac1{\beta\lambda_{\max}},
\qquad
\lambda_{\min}=\min\{\lambda_{0,\min},1/\beta\},
\quad
\lambda_{\max}=\max\{\lambda_{0,\max},1/\alpha\},
\end{equation}
for \emph{both} schemes, and sweep the fractions
$c\in\{0.05,0.1,0.25,0.5,0.75,1\}$ with $\Delta t=c\,\Delta t_{\mathrm{ref}}$, to
the same terminal time $T=15$ ($N=\lceil T/\Delta t\rceil$ steps). We do
\emph{not} use the tiny KL proof stepsize to size the grid; the stability study
already showed it is orders of magnitude smaller than the practically usable
range. The KL contraction formula is instead \emph{evaluated} on this common
grid, as a \emph{formal KL rate benchmark at Riemannian-scale stepsizes}---not a
claim that the current KL theorem has been proved for these stepsizes.

\paragraph{Contraction factors.}
For the Riemannian scheme the theorem per-step factor and per-unit-time rate are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:qriem}
q_{\mathrm{Riem}}(\Delta t)=1-\alpha\lambda_{\min}\Delta t\,(2-\beta\lambda_{\max}\Delta t),
\qquad
r_{\mathrm{Riem}}(\Delta t)=-\frac{\log q_{\mathrm{Riem}}(\Delta t)}{\Delta t}.
\end{equation}
On the grid $\Delta t\le\Delta t_{\mathrm{ref}}$ the bracket
$(2-\beta\lambda_{\max}\Delta t)\ge1$, so $q_{\mathrm{Riem}}\in(0,1)$. The KL
analysis gives, with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kappa}
\kappa(\Delta t)=\lambda_{\min}\min\!\Big\{
\frac{1+\Delta t\,\alpha\lambda_{\min}}{2(1+\Delta t)},\
\frac1{4(1+\Delta t\,\beta\lambda_{\max})^2}\Big\},
\end{equation}
the factor and rate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:qkl}
q_{\mathrm{KL}}(\Delta t)=1-2\alpha\,\kappa(\Delta t)\,\Delta t,
\qquad
r_{\mathrm{KL}}(\Delta t)=-\frac{\log q_{\mathrm{KL}}(\Delta t)}{\Delta t}.
\end{equation}
For reference, the continuous-time lower-bound rate is
$r_{\mathrm{cont}}=2\alpha\lambda_{\min}$. As $\Delta t\to0$ the Riemannian rate
$r_{\mathrm{Riem}}\to r_{\mathrm{cont}}$, whereas the KL formula rate tends to
$r_{\mathrm{cont}}/4$: at small $\Delta t$ the minimum in \eqref{eq:kappa} is
attained by the second term, $\kappa(0)=\lambda_{\min}/4$, so
$r_{\mathrm{KL}}\to\alpha\lambda_{\min}/2=r_{\mathrm{cont}}/4$. The KL formula is
thus a factor $\approx4$ more conservative than the Riemannian one even in the
small-stepsize limit, before any stepsize-dependent widening of the gap.

\paragraph{Observed contraction.}
For each run we record the full energy-gap trajectory and report a terminal
contraction factor
$\hat q_{\mathrm{term}}=(\mathrm{gap}_N/\mathrm{gap}_0)^{1/N}$ and rate
$\hat r_{\mathrm{term}}=-\log(\mathrm{gap}_N/\mathrm{gap}_0)/(N\Delta t)$, using a
small floor ($10^{-16}$) on the raw gap for the logarithm (the raw gap is kept in
the CSV, and floor-limited runs are flagged). We also fit a log-linear rate
$\hat r_{\mathrm{fit}}$ over the mid-regime
$10^{-10}\le\mathrm{gap}_n/\mathrm{gap}_0\le10^{-2}$, excluding the
machine-precision plateau. The \emph{terminal slack}
$q_{\mathrm{theory}}^{\,N}\mathrm{gap}_0/\mathrm{gap}_N$ measures how far the
method-specific theory envelope sits above the observed terminal gap.

\paragraph{Findings.}
Table~\ref{tab:disc-rate} aggregates the benchmark and
Figures~\ref{fig:rate-env-gauss}--\ref{fig:rate-itertol} display it.
Three observations hold consistently across both targets and all three $\lambda$.
First, the observed contraction is \emph{faster than either formula predicts}: in
the rate scatter (Fig.~\ref{fig:rate-scatter}) every point lies above the
$y=x$ diagonal, and the terminal slack is positive by several orders of magnitude
for every cell (Fig.~\ref{fig:rate-slack}, Table~\ref{tab:disc-rate}). Second,
the KL formula is the \emph{more conservative} of the two: at every $\Delta t$ on
the common grid $r_{\mathrm{KL}}(\Delta t)\le r_{\mathrm{Riem}}(\Delta t)$, so the
KL rate envelope predicts slower contraction and sits higher above the observed
curve, and the observed/theory rate ratio is correspondingly larger for KL
(Table~\ref{tab:disc-rate}: $\sim\!80$--$300\times$ for KL against
$\sim\!20$--$40\times$ for Riemannian). The terminal slack is large and positive
for \emph{both} schemes (several orders of magnitude); it is largest for KL at the
finer fractions, while at the coarsest fractions the Riemannian observed gap
descends to near machine precision (deeper observed convergence), which inflates
the Riemannian slack into the KL range there. Third, at the finest fractions ($c=0.05$) the two
\emph{observed} rates are nearly identical and close to the common continuous-time
value, consistent with both schemes being first-order discretizations of the same
flow; the Riemannian theoretical rate also approaches $r_{\mathrm{cont}}$ there
(from below), while the KL theoretical rate approaches only $r_{\mathrm{cont}}/4$
(eq.~\eqref{eq:kappa}), so both theoretical rates sit well below the observed rate
and the KL one further still. The iteration-to-tolerance comparison
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rate-itertol}) tells the same story from the cost side: where both
the observed and the theoretical iteration counts to gap $\le10^{-6}$ are finite,
$N_{\mathrm{theory}}$ exceeds $N_{\mathrm{obs}}$, and the KL theoretical count is
the largest. This strengthens the paper's central reading: at the
practically relevant Riemannian-scale stepsizes the KL disadvantage is in the
current \emph{proof}---both its stepsize bound and its rate formula are
conservative---not in the scheme's behavior. We are careful not to overclaim: the
KL formula evaluated here is a formal benchmark on a stepsize range the present KL
theorem does not establish, so the gap quantifies the conservativeness of the
current analysis, not a proved KL rate at these stepsizes.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

The evidence supports a balanced reading, not a simple ranking.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep4pt
  \item \textbf{The Riemannian discretization is the geometrically faithful one.}
  Its update \eqref{eq:riem} is exactly a geodesic step of the affine-invariant
  geometry, with the cleanest interpretation as geodesic gradient descent, and its
  theoretical stepsize bound is comparatively tight (empirical/theory ratio
  $2$--$200$). Its cost is the matrix exponential, immaterial here but the more
  expensive update in general.
  \item \textbf{The KL discretization is computationally simpler and empirically
  stable far beyond its proof bound.} The update \eqref{eq:kl} is a single
  symmetric solve, and on the globally smooth targets it is stable and monotone for
  stepsizes $10^{4}$--$10^{8}$ times larger than the current sufficient condition
  permits (Table~\ref{tab:disc-step}, Fig.~\ref{fig:theory-vs-emp}).
  \item \textbf{The KL stepsize restriction is conservative.} The large and
  systematic gap between the empirical and theoretical KL limits---of the same
  order as the $\lambda_{\max}^3/(2\lambda_{\min}^3)$ factor that distinguishes the
  two proofs---indicates that this factor is an artifact of the bounding argument
  rather than a genuine restriction on the scheme. It persists under the strict
  accuracy criterion, not just bare stability (Table~\ref{tab:disc-step}).
  \item \textbf{The KL covariance update is not the instability source.} For a
  log-concave target the KL precision $C^{-1}-\Delta t\,H$ is SPD for every
  $\Delta t>0$, so the covariance step never leaves the SPD cone; the scalar
  diagnostic (Fig.~\ref{fig:scalar}) confirms unconditional contraction of the
  covariance to its fixed point.
  \item \textbf{Large-stepsize failures come from the shared mean update.} Both
  schemes diverge at large $\Delta t$ through the explicit mean step
  $m_{n+1}=(1-\Delta t\,C_n)m_n$, which overshoots once $\Delta t\,C_n>2$
  regardless of how the covariance is advanced. This is why the two schemes' SPD
  and stability boundaries coincide on the quartic target and why the absolute
  stepsize ceiling is set by the mean, not the covariance.
  \item \textbf{At a matched stepsize the Riemannian scheme usually converges
  faster.} On both globally smooth targets the geodesic update reaches a smaller
  terminal energy gap than KL at every convergent stepsize, with the margin growing
  from a small factor at fine $\Delta t$ to $10^{4}$--$10^{8}$ at the coarsest
  convergent stepsizes (Sec.~\ref{app:speed}, Table~\ref{tab:disc-conv}); KL leads
  only on the non-smooth quartic and only modestly. So when one operates at a
  stepsize chosen as large as stability allows---the practical regime---the
  Riemannian update is typically the faster choice.
\end{itemize}
We are careful not to overclaim. At well-conditioned settings the absolute stable
stepsize of the two schemes is comparable (indeed at $\lambda=1$ the Riemannian
scheme tolerates a slightly larger stable $\Delta t$ than KL), and on the
non-smooth quartic target both are restricted to small stepsizes, where KL is the
faster of the two. The conclusions are specifically about the
\emph{gap between the KL proof bound and KL practice} (large and systematic) and
the \emph{matched-stepsize convergence-speed advantage of the Riemannian update on
smooth targets}, not a blanket statement that one scheme dominates the other in
every respect.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This is numerical evidence on $d\le2$ deterministic targets, not a theorem. The
stepsize grid is coarse (factors of $2$--$2.5$), so the reported empirical maxima
are accurate only to within one grid step; the qualitative four-to-six order gap
is far larger than this resolution. Establishing that the KL scheme admits
Riemannian-sized stepsizes in general requires a revised proof; the present data
indicate where to look (the $\lambda_{\max}^3/\lambda_{\min}^3$ factor) and that
the shared mean step, not the covariance update, is the true stepsize bottleneck.
