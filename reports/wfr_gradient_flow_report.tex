\title{Wasserstein--Fisher--Rao Gaussian Gradient Flow:\\
Phase Separation Between Transport and Natural-Gradient Descent}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study the Wasserstein--Fisher--Rao (WFR) gradient flow on the Gaussian
variational manifold, a hybrid of the Bures--Wasserstein transport flow and the
Fisher--Rao (natural-gradient) flow with a tunable transport strength
$\lambda_t$. We discretize the flow by forward--backward operator splitting --
one Wasserstein half-step followed by one Fisher--Rao half-step per iteration --
and compare it against the two pure flows ($\lambda\equiv 0$, Fisher--Rao only;
and Wasserstein only) on two ill-conditioned two-dimensional targets: an exact
anisotropic Gaussian posterior and a smooth nonseparable strongly log-concave
posterior. From a far, underdispersed initialization the experiments expose a
clean \emph{phase separation}: the Wasserstein transport drives fast early
descent (warmup) but stalls in the tail, while the Fisher--Rao step is slow
during warmup but converges rapidly once the covariance is locally calibrated to
the curvature. The WFR splitting inherits both phases, and a curvature-adaptive
transport schedule $h_n = h_{\max}/(1+(s_n/s_0)^2)$ -- large transport when the
state is underdispersed, decaying transport as the covariance calibrates --
realizes the hand-off automatically.

Measured against the energy gap versus iteration, the verdict is decisive. The
WFR splitting reaches every tolerance in roughly half the iterations of pure
Fisher--Rao: the curvature-adaptive schedule reaches gap $<10^{-3}$ in $15$--$22$
iterations against Fisher--Rao's $31$--$41$, and dominates the fixed and
theorem-bound schedules among WFR variants -- the theorem bound $h=\mu_{\min}$ is
far too conservative once the state is underdispersed. Wasserstein-only is
decisively worst: it collapses the bulk of the initial gap in the first few
iterations but then plateaus, taking thousands of iterations to reach $10^{-6}$
(and never reaching it on the Gaussian target at $\Lambda=1000$) because
transport alone inflates the covariance volume without ever calibrating its
shape.
\end{abstract}

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Gaussian variational family and energy.}
We approximate a target posterior $\rho_\post \propto e^{-V}$ on $\R^d$ ($d=2$
throughout) by a Gaussian $\N(m,C)$, $C\in\SPD(d)$, parameterized by the state
$a=(m,C)$. The variational objective is the (reverse) KL up to an
$a$-independent constant,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:energy}
\mathcal{E}(a) \;=\; \E_{\theta\sim\N(m,C)}[V(\theta)] \;-\; \tfrac12\log\det C ,
\qquad
\mathcal{E}(a) - \mathcal{E}(a_\star) \;=\; \KL\!\big(\N(m,C)\,\big\|\,\rho_\post\big),
\end{equation}
minimized at $a_\star=(m_\star,C_\star)$. We write the posterior log-gradient
and log-Hessian expectations
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gH}
g \;=\; \E_{\N(m,C)}[\grad\log\rho_\post] \;=\; -\,\E[\grad V],
\qquad
H \;=\; \E_{\N(m,C)}[\grad^2\log\rho_\post] \;=\; -\,\E[\grad^2 V],
\end{equation}
so that $-H=\E[\grad^2 V]\succeq 0$ for a log-concave target. We assume global
strong log-concavity and smoothness, $\alpha I \preceq \grad^2 V \preceq \beta I$.

\paragraph{The WFR Gaussian flow.}
The Bures--Wasserstein gradient flow of $\mathcal{E}$ moves mass by transport; the
Fisher--Rao flow rescales mass by the natural gradient. The WFR flow combines
them with transport strength $\lambda_t\ge 0$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wfr-flow}
\dot m \;=\; (C+\lambda_t I)\,g, \qquad
\dot C \;=\; \underbrace{C + CHC}_{\text{Fisher--Rao}}
\;+\; \lambda_t\underbrace{\big(2I + CH + HC\big)}_{\text{Wasserstein}} .
\end{equation}
Setting $\lambda_t\equiv 0$ recovers the Fisher--Rao (natural-gradient) flow;
dropping the Fisher--Rao terms recovers the Bures--Wasserstein flow. The
additive $2\lambda_t I$ term is the source of the warmup advantage analyzed
below: it inflates an underdispersed covariance at a rate independent of the
current scale.

\paragraph{Targets.}
The anisotropy parameter is $\Lambda$ (the symbol $\lambda$ is reserved for the
transport strength $\lambda_t$). We use two targets.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Gaussian posterior:} $\rho_\post=\N(0,\Gamma)$ with
$\Gamma=\mathrm{diag}(1,\Lambda)$ the \emph{covariance}. Then
$V(\theta)=\tfrac12\theta^\top\Gamma^{-1}\theta$, so $g=-\Gamma^{-1}m$ and
$H=-\Gamma^{-1}$ are exact and state-independent, $\alpha=1/\Lambda$, $\beta=1$,
and the optimum is closed form $m_\star=0$, $C_\star=\Gamma$ with an analytic
energy gap \eqref{eq:energy}.
\item \textbf{Smooth log-cosh posterior:} the nonseparable strongly log-concave
\begin{equation}
V(x,y) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{20}\big(\sqrt{\Lambda}\,x - y\big)^2
\;+\; \tfrac{\delta}{2}\,(x^2+y^2)
\;+\; \gamma\,\log\cosh(y),
\quad \delta=0.05,\ \gamma=1 ,
\end{equation}
with $\alpha=\delta$ and the eigenvalue smoothness constant
$\beta=\lambda_{\max}\!\big(\begin{smallmatrix}\delta+\Lambda/10 &
-\sqrt{\Lambda}/10\\ -\sqrt{\Lambda}/10 & \delta+1/10+\gamma\end{smallmatrix}\big)$.
Expectations of $\tanh$, $\mathrm{sech}^2$ and $\log\cosh$ under the marginal
$Y\sim\N(m_y,C_{22})$ are evaluated by deterministic Gauss--Hermite quadrature;
the optimum is found numerically.
\end{itemize}
The target metadata, including $\beta/\alpha$ (the condition number that governs
the tail rate), is reported in Table~\ref{tab:wfr-metadata}.

\paragraph{Initialization.}
Every run starts far and underdispersed: $C_0=\varepsilon I$ and the mean placed
a distance $r=8$ along the flat-valley direction
$(1,\sqrt{\Lambda})/\sqrt{1+\Lambda}$. This is the regime that separates the two
flows -- the covariance must inflate by orders of magnitude and the mean must
traverse the valley.

\section{Forward--backward discretization and the five methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Operator splitting.}
We discretize \eqref{eq:wfr-flow} by forward--backward operator splitting with
Fisher--Rao step $\Delta t$ and Wasserstein step $h_n=\lambda_n\,\Delta t$. Each
iteration applies one Wasserstein half-step then one Fisher--Rao half-step.
With $g_n,H_n$ from \eqref{eq:gH} evaluated at $a_n=(m_n,C_n)$, the
\emph{Wasserstein half-step} is
\begin{align}
\label{eq:w-step}
m_{n+1/2} &= m_n + h_n\,g_n, &
M_n &= I + h_n H_n, \qquad \widetilde C = M_n C_n M_n, \notag\\
C_{n+1/2} &= \tfrac12\Big(\widetilde C + 2h_n I
+ \big[\widetilde C(\widetilde C + 4h_n I)\big]^{1/2}\Big),
\end{align}
and, with $g_{n+1/2},H_{n+1/2}$ re-evaluated at $a_{n+1/2}$, the
\emph{Fisher--Rao half-step} is the KL/natural-gradient update
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fr-step}
m_{n+1} = m_{n+1/2} + \Delta t\,C_{n+1/2}\,g_{n+1/2}, \qquad
C_{n+1} = (1+\Delta t)\big(C_{n+1/2}^{-1} - \Delta t\,H_{n+1/2}\big)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The matrix square root in \eqref{eq:w-step} is computed spectrally: $\widetilde
C$ and $\widetilde C+4h_nI$ commute, so with the symmetric eigendecomposition
$\widetilde C=V\,\mathrm{diag}(w)\,V^\top$,
\begin{equation}
C_{n+1/2} = V\,\mathrm{diag}\!\Big(\tfrac12\big(w + 2h_n
+ \sqrt{w(w+4h_n)}\big)\Big)\,V^\top,
\end{equation}
which is exact, symmetric, and SPD whenever $\widetilde C\succeq 0$ and
$h_n\ge 0$. The Fisher--Rao covariance update \eqref{eq:fr-step} is
unconditionally SPD for log-concave targets ($-H\succeq0$). At $h_n=0$ the
Wasserstein half-step is the identity and the iteration reduces exactly to the
pure Fisher--Rao step.

\paragraph{The five methods.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{FR-only} ($\lambda\equiv0$): pure Fisher--Rao, $h_n=0$.
\item \textbf{W-only}: the Wasserstein half-step \eqref{eq:w-step} alone with the
fixed $h_n=c/\beta$.
\item \textbf{WFR-fixed}: the full splitting with fixed $h_n=c/\beta$.
\item \textbf{WFR-theory}: the full splitting with the constant
$h_n=\mu_{\min}=\min(\lambda_{\min}(C_0),1/\beta)$, the maximizer of the proven
discrete transport contribution $f(h)=\alpha h/(1+h/\mu_{\min})^2$.
\item \textbf{WFR-adaptive}: the full splitting with the curvature-adaptive
schedule
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:adaptive}
h_n = \frac{h_{\max}}{1+(s_n/s_0)^2}, \quad
h_{\max}=\frac{0.9}{\beta}, \quad s_0=0.5, \quad
s_n = \lambda_{\min}\!\big(C_n^{1/2}(-H_n)\,C_n^{1/2}\big).
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}
The calibration ratio $s_n$ is the smallest eigenvalue of the dimensionless
curvature-in-covariance-metric operator: $s_n\ll1$ signals an underdispersed
state (the Fisher--Rao step is throttled, large transport helps warmup), while
$s_n\approx1$ signals a locally calibrated covariance (the Fisher--Rao step is
reliable, transport should decay). For the Gaussian target at $C=\Gamma$,
$-H=\Gamma^{-1}$ gives $C^{1/2}(-H)C^{1/2}=I$ and $s_n=1$ exactly.

\subsection{Phase separation: transport wins warmup, Fisher--Rao wins the tail}

Figures~\ref{fig:wfr-phase-gaussian}--\ref{fig:wfr-phase-smooth} show the energy
gap against iteration, with a warmup zoom (left) and the full
trajectory (right). Three facts are consistent across every $(\Lambda,
\varepsilon)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{W-only descends fastest in the first few iterations} but
\emph{plateaus}: from $C_0=\varepsilon I$ the additive $2h_nI$ term in
\eqref{eq:w-step} inflates the covariance at a scale-independent rate, collapsing
the bulk of the initial gap quickly, after which the lack of the
natural-gradient rescaling leaves it stalled far from $a_\star$.
\item \textbf{FR-only is slow during warmup} -- the natural-gradient covariance
inflates only geometrically and the mean update $\Delta t\,C g$ is throttled by
the tiny $C$ -- \textbf{but converges fastest among the pure flows in the tail}
once the covariance is calibrated.
\item \textbf{WFR (fixed and adaptive) inherit both phases}: they track W-only's
fast warmup and then FR-only's fast tail. This makes them
the fastest methods overall -- the adaptive schedule reaches gap $<10^{-3}$ in
$15$--$22$ iterations, about half of FR-only's $31$--$41$
(Table~\ref{tab:wfr-iters}), and reaches $10^{-6}$ in $23$--$35$ iterations
across the whole grid.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The mechanism: the covariance, not the mean, is the bottleneck}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The phase separation is not just a property of the scalar energy gap -- it has a
concrete mechanism, visible once the gap is decomposed into its mean and
covariance parts. Figure~\ref{fig:wfr-decomp} plots $\|m_n-m_\star\|$ and
$\|C_n-C_\star\|_F$ separately on the hardest start ($\Lambda=1000$,
$\varepsilon=10^{-3}$). Three observations pin down why the flows behave as they
do:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The covariance dominates the error budget.} On the Gaussian target
the covariance error at the underdispersed start is $\approx\!10^{3}$ (it must
inflate from $\varepsilon I$ to a top eigenvalue $\Lambda=10^3$) while the mean
error is only $\approx\!8$. The optimization is, to leading order, a
covariance-inflation problem; the mean is a sideshow.
\item \textbf{The mean always calibrates before the covariance.} For every
convergent method the mean error crosses $10^{-3}$ tens of iterations before the
covariance error does (e.g.\ on the smooth target, FR-only at $39$ vs $52$
iterations; WFR-adaptive at $24$ vs $42$). The tail rate is therefore set
entirely by how fast the covariance is calibrated -- which is exactly the
quantity the Fisher--Rao step accelerates and the adaptive ratio $s_n$ measures.
\item \textbf{W-only inflates volume but cannot calibrate shape.} On the Gaussian
target its covariance error barely moves (it drifts from $\approx\!10^3$ down to
only $\approx\!9.5\times 10^2$ over the first $60$ iterations) because the
additive $2h_nI$ term grows the volume isotropically without ever matching the
target's anisotropy. This is the mechanism behind its plateau: transport produces
a big but mis-shaped covariance, and only the natural-gradient rescaling can fix
the shape.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:wfr-adaptive} shows the normalized transport $h_n\beta$ and the

near the ceiling $h_{\max}\beta=0.9$ while $s_n\ll s_0$ (the underdispersed

\subsection{Iterations to convergence}
\label{sec:iters}

Table~\ref{tab:wfr-iters} reports, for the harder underdispersed start
$\varepsilon=10^{-3}$, the iterations each method needs to reach
$\mathrm{gap}<10^{-3}$, and Table~\ref{tab:wfr-hitting} gives the full
per-run breakdown across thresholds $10^{-1}$, $10^{-3}$, $10^{-6}$.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The WFR splitting wins outright.} WFR-adaptive reaches the
tolerance in $15$--$22$ iterations against FR-only's $31$--$41$ -- roughly half,
because each iteration blends a transport move and a natural-gradient move -- and
WFR-fixed is close behind. The advantage is largest at the harder, more
underdispersed starts, where the transport warmup matters most.
\item \textbf{W-only is decisively worst.} It collapses the bulk of the gap in
the first handful of iterations but then plateaus far from the optimum, taking
$575$--$3653$ iterations to reach $10^{-3}$ and never reaching $10^{-6}$ on the
Gaussian target at $\Lambda=1000$ within the budget
(Table~\ref{tab:wfr-hitting}); transport alone cannot calibrate the covariance.
\end{itemize}
All twenty production runs per method are SPD-feasible and monotone throughout.

\subsection{How much transport? The schedule sweep}

Figure~\ref{fig:wfr-sweep} sweeps the fixed fraction $c$ (so $h=c/\beta$, with
$c=0$ recovering FR-only); Table~\ref{tab:wfr-sweep} gives the numbers.
Increasing $c$ \emph{monotonically reduces the iteration count} until it
saturates: more transport means each iteration makes more progress through the
warmup. The theorem-bound schedule $h=\mu_{\min}$ (star markers) lands in the
interior of the fixed-$c$ curve rather than at an optimum: with the tiny
$C_0=\varepsilon I$ the bound gives $\mu_{\min}=\varepsilon$ on the Gaussian
target (since $\beta=1$), an extremely small transport that throttles the warmup
the bound was meant to accelerate -- direct evidence that the conservative
proof-bound schedule underuses transport precisely in the underdispersed regime
it targets.

\subsection{Stability across the discretization step}

The appendix heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:wfr-dt}) sweeps the Fisher--Rao step

splitting remains SPD-feasible and convergent across a broad joint range rather

\section{Findings and implications}
\label{sec:findings}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The two flows phase-separate.} Wasserstein transport, through its
scale-independent $2h_nI$ covariance inflation, dominates the warmup; Fisher--Rao
natural-gradient descent dominates the tail once the covariance is calibrated to
the curvature. The separation is clean and holds across both targets and all
$(\Lambda,\varepsilon)$.
\item \textbf{The WFR splitting combines the phases, halving iterations.} It
reaches every tolerance in roughly half the iterations of pure Fisher--Rao
(Table~\ref{tab:wfr-iters}): WFR-adaptive hits gap $<10^{-3}$ in $15$--$22$
iterations against FR-only's $31$--$41$, and reaches $10^{-6}$ in $23$--$35$.
This is the headline result -- combining transport and natural-gradient descent
in one iteration is strictly faster than either pure flow.
\item \textbf{Adaptivity beats both fixed schedules, and the proof bound is too
conservative.} Among WFR variants the curvature-adaptive schedule dominates fixed
$c$ and the theorem bound $h=\mu_{\min}$; the latter collapses to a negligible
transport in exactly the underdispersed regime it was meant to accelerate.
\item \textbf{Transport alone is not enough.} W-only plateaus and never reaches
$10^{-6}$ at $\Lambda=1000$ on the Gaussian target: without the natural-gradient
rescaling it cannot calibrate the covariance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The study is deliberately controlled and its scope bounds the claims.
\emph{(i) Dimension and targets:} $d=2$, two globally strongly-log-concave
targets; the per-iteration speedup constants will differ in higher dimension
and the phase separation is not established for multimodal or heavy-tailed
targets. \emph{(ii) Exact expectations:} $(g,H)$ are exact (closed form or
Gauss--Hermite), so the comparison isolates the discretization, not Monte-Carlo
gradient noise; under sampling noise the relative ranking could shift.
\emph{(iii) First-order splitting:} we use one Wasserstein then one Fisher--Rao
half-step (Lie--Trotter); a Strang splitting or a single fused step could change
the per-iteration progress and is untested. \emph{(iv) The adaptive schedule}
is one heuristic with hand-set $h_{\max}=0.9/\beta$, $s_0=0.5$; we do not claim
it is optimal, only that it dominates the fixed and proof-bound schedules tested.
