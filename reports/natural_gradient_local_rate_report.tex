\title{The Local Convergence Rate of Gaussian Natural Gradient Flow\\
is Empirically Dimension-Free}
\author{}

\begin{abstract}
The Gaussian natural gradient flow contracts toward its equilibrium at a local
rate $\gloc$ equal to the smallest eigenvalue of the linearized positive
generator $L_\star$ in the Fisher--Rao metric. The current analysis bounds
$\gloc$ from below by $1/(4+\Gamma)$ with
$\Gamma=\min\{\beta-1,\,n(3+\tfrac{4}{\sqrt\pi}(1+\log\kappa))\}$, whose second
branch carries an explicit dimension factor $n=N_\theta$. We estimate $\gloc$
directly from the linearized operator over a controlled grid
($N_\theta=1,\dots,16$; $\kappa\in\{2,5,10,20,50,100\}$; five potential families;
three seeds; $M=2^{22}$ Monte Carlo samples per point, float64, GPU). Across
every family the measured $\gloc$ is essentially flat in $N_\theta$ and varies
only with the conditioning $\kappa$. This indicates that the genuine local rate
is governed by $\kappa$ rather than $N_\theta$, and that the $N_\theta$ factor in
the present bound is an artifact of the proof rather than a property of the flow.
We are careful to separate this conclusion, which rests on the well-conditioned
estimator $\gloc$, from the full-operator norm estimate $\Lhat$, whose apparent
growth with $N_\theta$ we show to be finite-sample spectral noise.
\end{abstract}

\section{Setup}

Work in equilibrium-whitened coordinates, where the target satisfies
$\E_{\N(0,I)}[\grad V(Z)]=0$ and $\E_{\N(0,I)}[\grad^2 V(Z)]=I$, so the
equilibrium of the Gaussian natural gradient flow is $a_\star=(0,I)$.
Parameterize a perturbation as $m=u$ and $C=I+X$ with $X\in\Sym(N_\theta)$.

\begin{definition}[Linearized operators]\label{def:ops}
For $u\in\R^{N_\theta}$ and $X\in\Sym(N_\theta)$, with $Z\sim\N(0,I)$ and
$W(Z)=\grad^2 V(Z)$, define
\begin{align*}
T[X]&=\E\big[\Tr(X\,W(Z))\,Z\big], &
T^\ast[u]&=\E\big[W(Z)\,(Z^\top u)\big], \\
H[X]&=\E\big[W(Z)\,(Z^\top X Z-\Tr X)\big], &
H_{\mathrm{sym}}[X]&=\tfrac12\big(H[X]+H^\ast[X]\big).
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Linearized generator]\label{prop:Lstar}
The linearized positive generator $L_\star=-J_\star$ of the flow is
\begin{equation}
L_\star(u,X)=\Big(u+\tfrac12 T[X],\;\; X+T^\ast[u]+\tfrac12 H_{\mathrm{sym}}[X]\Big),
\end{equation}
and it is self-adjoint with respect to the local Fisher--Rao inner product at
equilibrium,
\begin{equation*}
\langle(u_1,X_1),(u_2,X_2)\rangle_\star=u_1^\top u_2+\tfrac12\Tr(X_1X_2).
\end{equation*}
The local convergence rate is the smallest eigenvalue
$\gloc=\lambda_{\min}(L_\star)$.
\end{proposition}

The analysis of the manuscript establishes the lower bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bound}
\gloc\;\ge\;\frac{1}{4+\Gamma},
\qquad
\Gamma=\min\Big\{\beta-1,\;N_\theta\big(3+\tfrac{4}{\sqrt\pi}(1+\log\kappa)\big)\Big\},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha\le 1\le\beta$ are the strong-convexity and smoothness constants and
$\kappa=\beta/\alpha$. The dimension-free conjecture replaces $\Gamma$ by
$D_\kappa=(4+\tfrac{4}{\sqrt\pi})(1+\log\kappa)$, i.e. a rate that depends on the
conditioning $\kappa$ but not on $N_\theta$. The experiments below test which
behavior the measured $\gloc$ actually follows.

\section{Estimator and experimental design}

\paragraph{Estimator.}
The operators of Definition~\ref{def:ops} are estimated on a fixed Monte Carlo
bank $\{Z_j\}_{j=1}^{M}$, $Z_j\sim\N(0,I)$. Two corrections are essential for the
spectrum to be meaningful on a finite bank.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Symmetrization.} In exact expectation $H$ is self-adjoint, but the
  plain forward Monte Carlo estimator is not; its Frobenius adjoint is a
  different operator. We estimate the explicitly symmetrized
  $H_{\mathrm{sym}}=\tfrac12(H_{\mathrm{fwd}}+H_{\mathrm{adj}})$ on the same bank
  and use it for every eigenvalue. The residual self-adjointness error is at the
  level of $10^{-15}$.
  \item \emph{Fisher--Rao packing.} $L_\star$ is self-adjoint in the Fisher--Rao
  metric, not the Euclidean one. Packing tangents as
  $y=(u,\ \mathrm{sym\_to\_vec}(X)/\sqrt2)$, with $\mathrm{sym\_to\_vec}$ a
  Frobenius isometry, makes the packed matrix genuinely symmetric, and $\gloc$ is
  obtained from a dense symmetric eigensolve.
\end{itemize}
The revised proof route also records the first-Hermite coupling
\begin{equation*}
    \tau_H^2=\|T\|_{\mathrm{op}}^2
    =
    \sup_{\|w\|=1}
    \left\|
    \E[\grad^2 V(Z)(w^\top Z)]
    \right\|_F^2 .
\end{equation*}
This is the quantity that controls the mean-covariance coupling in the
linearized generator. The deterministic reduction
$\gloc\ge 1/(\tau_H^2+3)$ separates this coupling channel from large positive
covariance-curvature modes, which may increase $\Lhat$ without slowing the flow.
The same Bochner-Schur identity and the upper Hessian bound give the baseline
$\tau_H^2\le\beta$, hence $\gloc\ge 1/(\beta+3)\ge 1/(\kappa+3)$ in whitened
coordinates. The remaining question is whether $\beta$ can be replaced by
$O(1+\log\kappa)$.
The implementation also decomposes the top singular mode of $T$ into the
longitudinal, mixed, and transverse blocks of Section~3, so future adversarial
runs can identify whether large coupling is entering through the only unresolved
high-rank transverse channel.
For separable control potentials a Gauss--Hermite quadrature provides an exact,
sample-free benchmark $\Lambda_{\mathrm{exact}}=\max_i\E[V_i''(Z)(Z^2-1)]$ that is
dimension-free by construction; it is used below to expose finite-sample noise in
the full-operator estimate.

\paragraph{Potential families.}
All non-Gaussian targets are built by the same construction: a raw feature
$\Phi$ is centered so that $\E_{\N(0,I)}[\grad V]=0$ and
$\E_{\N(0,I)}[\grad^2 V]=I$, giving $V=\tfrac12\|\theta\|^2+\rho(\Phi-b^\top\theta-\tfrac12\theta^\top M\theta)$
with $\grad^2 V=I+\rho(\grad^2\Phi-M)$. The coupling $\rho$ is set from the target
conditioning $\kappa$ through $\rho=\tfrac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}/L_A$, so that
$\alpha=\tfrac{2}{\kappa+1}$, $\beta=\tfrac{2\kappa}{\kappa+1}$, and
$\beta/\alpha=\kappa$. The five families are chosen to vary one structural
property of $\grad^2 V$ at a time, so that any genuine dimension dependence would
have to survive across qualitatively different Hessian geometries:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep3pt
  \item \textbf{Gaussian} ($V=\tfrac12\|\theta\|^2$, $\grad^2 V=I$). The exact
  control: the equilibrium is reached identically, the true operators vanish, and
  $\gloc=1$ analytically. It calibrates the estimator and the finite-sample floor,
  since any departure from $\Lambda=0$, $\gloc=1$ is pure Monte Carlo noise.
  \item \textbf{Separable}, $\Phi(\theta)=\sum_i\phi(\theta_i+c_i)$ with
  $\phi=\log\cosh$. The Hessian is \emph{diagonal}, so the linearized operator
  decouples coordinate-wise and the rate is governed by the one-dimensional
  curvature law $\E[V_i''(Z)(Z^2-1)]$. This is the only family with a
  closed-form, dimension-free benchmark (Gauss--Hermite), making it the reference
  case: if the rate were to acquire an $N_\theta$ factor, the diagonal family
  would expose it most cleanly. It is also the most strongly conditioned in
  practice (smallest realized $\gloc$).
  \item \textbf{Additive-index}, $\Phi(\theta)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt r}\sum_{\ell=1}^{r}a_\ell\,\phi(w_\ell^\top\theta+c_\ell)$
  with $r=4N_\theta$ random unit directions $w_\ell$. The Hessian
  $\grad^2\Phi=\sum_\ell\text{(scalar)}\,w_\ell w_\ell^\top$ is a sum of rank-one
  terms along fixed directions; it carries genuine off-diagonal curvature, so the
  linearized operator no longer decouples. This is the first test of whether
  coupling between coordinates introduces dimension dependence.
  \item \textbf{Random-feature}: the same ridge-sum form with a dense bank of
  random directions and width growing as $r=4N_\theta$. It is the general
  coupled potential and the primary stress test---the Hessian is full and its
  spectrum is determined by the random-feature geometry rather than a small set
  of indices.
  \item \textbf{Radial-tail}, $\Phi(\theta)=h(\|\theta\|^2)$ with
  $h=\log\cosh$. The Hessian $2h'I+4h''\theta\theta^\top$ is isotropic in
  distribution, so the perturbation lives almost entirely in the trace/volume
  mode. It probes the heavy-tailed, rotationally symmetric regime; because the
  isotropic mode is exactly the channel $L_\star$ contracts fastest, it serves as
  a second (non-separable) near-ideal control.
\end{itemize}
These families span the relevant axes---no coupling (Gaussian, separable),
sparse directional coupling (additive-index), dense coupling (random-feature),
and isotropic heavy-tail (radial-tail)---so that a dimension-dependent rate would
have to appear in at least one of them if it were real.

\paragraph{Final production grid.}
All results in this report come from a single production run; smoke, baseline,
and high-dimensional pilot runs are explicitly excluded. The grid spans five
potential families (the analytic Gaussian; separable; additive-index;
random-feature; radial-tail), $N_\theta=1,\dots,16$, six conditioning values
$\kappa$, and three seeds, with $M=2^{22}$ Monte Carlo samples per grid point in
float64 on a single GPU. Every one of the $1440$ rows completed successfully.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{$\gloc$ is flat in $N_\theta$.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gamma-N} shows $\gloc$ as a function of $N_\theta$ at the hardest
conditioning $\kappa=100$, for each family (mean over seeds, shaded
$\pm$ standard deviation). For every family the curve is essentially horizontal
across $N_\theta=1,\dots,16$: the Gaussian and radial-tail controls sit at
$\gloc\approx1$, the coupled additive-index and random-feature families hold near
$0.91$, and the separable family near $0.73$, with no downward drift in
$N_\theta$. This is the central observation: the measured rate does not decay
with dimension, in contrast to the $1/(4+\Gamma)$ branch of \eqref{eq:bound},
whose $N_\theta$ factor would force $\gloc\to0$ as $N_\theta$ grows.

\paragraph{$\gloc$ varies with $\kappa$.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gamma-kappa} fixes $N_\theta=16$ and varies $\kappa$. Here the
coupled families do move: the separable rate decreases from $\approx0.91$ at
$\kappa=2$ to $\approx0.73$ at $\kappa=100$, and the additive-index/random-feature
families from $\approx0.97$ to $\approx0.91$, while the Gaussian and radial-tail
controls remain at $\approx1$. The dependence is mild and monotone in $\log\kappa$,
consistent with the conditioning-driven $D_\kappa$ form rather than a dimensional
one. Table~\ref{tab:lr-ranges} gives the per-family ranges over the whole grid.

\paragraph{Per-family reading.}
The ordering of the families by $\gloc$ follows directly from their Hessian
geometry, which is the consistency check that the numbers are measuring the
intended effect.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep3pt
  \item The \emph{Gaussian} and \emph{radial-tail} families sit at
  $\gloc\approx1$ throughout. For the Gaussian this is exact; for radial-tail the
  perturbation is isotropic and therefore lives in the trace/volume mode, which
  is precisely the channel $L_\star$ contracts at unit rate. Their flatness in
  both $N_\theta$ and $\kappa$ confirms the estimator is unbiased where the answer
  is known.
  \item The \emph{separable} family has the smallest rate (down to
  $\gloc\approx0.65$) and the strongest $\kappa$-dependence. This is expected: it
  is the most strongly conditioned realization (at $\kappa=100$, $N_\theta=8$ the
  centering gives $\rho\approx1.55$, $\alpha\approx0.02$, $\beta\approx1.98$), and
  its diagonal Hessian concentrates the curvature deficit in the shape modes that
  $L_\star$ contracts slowest. Even so $\gloc$ is flat in $N_\theta$.
  \item The \emph{additive-index} and \emph{random-feature} families track each
  other closely ($\gloc\approx0.91$ at $\kappa=100$) and lie between the controls
  and the separable case. The off-diagonal curvature they introduce lowers the
  rate relative to the isotropic controls but---crucially---adds no $N_\theta$
  dependence, even as the feature width grows with dimension ($r=4N_\theta$).
\end{itemize}
In every family the dependence is on $\kappa$, through how aggressively the
target is conditioned, and not on $N_\theta$.

\paragraph{Relation to the bound.}
Two remarks place these measurements against \eqref{eq:bound}. First, $\gloc$
stays well above the lower bound $1/(4+\Gamma)$ everywhere (the smallest measured
$\gloc\approx0.63$ versus a bound $\le0.25$), as it must. Second, in this
construction the smoothness satisfies $\beta=2\kappa/(\kappa+1)<2$, so the
$\beta-1$ branch of $\Gamma$ is the active one for $N_\theta\ge2$; the
experiments therefore do not drive the $N_\theta(3+\tfrac{4}{\sqrt\pi}(1+\log\kappa))$
branch through $\beta$. What they test is the complementary and more basic
question---whether the \emph{realized} rate carries any $N_\theta$ dependence at
all---and the answer over the tested range is that it does not.

\paragraph{The full-operator norm is a noisy diagnostic, not the rate.}
A natural temptation is to read dimension dependence off the largest eigenvalue
$\Lhat$ of the symmetric-matrix operator $H_{\mathrm{sym}}$. This is misleading.
$H_{\mathrm{sym}}$ acts on the $N_\theta(N_\theta+1)/2$-dimensional space of
symmetric matrices, so its top eigenvalue is biased upward by finite-sample
spectral noise that grows with that dimension. Figure~\ref{fig:lambda-artifact}
makes this explicit for the separable family, where the Gauss--Hermite benchmark
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{exact}}$ is exact and dimension-free: the Monte Carlo estimate
$\Lhat_{\mathrm{full\;sym}}$ rises with $N_\theta$ while $\Lambda_{\mathrm{exact}}$
is flat. The gap is estimator noise, not a property of the flow, which is why the
dimension-independence conclusion is drawn from the well-conditioned $\gloc$
(a smallest-eigenvalue problem on the metric-correct operator) and not from
$\Lhat$.
In the revised diagnostics, $\tau_H^2$ is tracked separately: unlike $\Lhat$, it
measures only the first-Hermite mean-covariance coupling, and the row-wise check
$\gloc\ge 1/(\tau_H^2+3)$ is an algebraic consequence of the Bochner-Schur
identity on the same operator.

\section{Discussion}

The measured local rate $\gloc$ is flat in $N_\theta$ at every conditioning and
moves only with $\kappa$ (Figures~\ref{fig:gamma-N}--\ref{fig:gamma-grid}). This
is the behavior of the dimension-free form $1/(4+D_\kappa)$ and not of the
$1/(4+n(\cdots))$ branch of the present bound \eqref{eq:bound}. The numerical
evidence therefore indicates that the genuine local convergence rate of Gaussian
natural gradient flow is governed by the conditioning $\kappa$, and that the
explicit $N_\theta$ factor in the current proof is an artifact of the bounding
argument---most plausibly the step that controls $\lambda_{\max}(G-\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top)$
by $N_\theta(3+\tfrac{4}{\sqrt\pi}(1+\log\kappa))$ via a sum over all
coordinates, rather than a property of $L_\star$ itself.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This is numerical evidence, not a theorem. The grid covers $N_\theta\le16$ at a
single sample size $M=2^{22}$; while the separable diagonal and Gauss--Hermite
benchmarks confirm that $\gloc$ is well-converged at this $M$ (unlike $\Lhat$),
the experiments do not establish behavior for $N_\theta\gg16$. The conclusion is
that the data are inconsistent with a genuine $1/N_\theta$-type decay over the
tested range and consistent with a $\kappa$-only rate; turning this into a
dimension-free guarantee requires a revised proof. The full-operator norm $\Lhat$
is reported only as a diagnostic and is explicitly not used as evidence about the
rate.
