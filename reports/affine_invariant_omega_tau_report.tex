\title{Affine-Invariant Gaussian Gradient Flows:\\
Mode Decomposition and the Role of $(\omega,\tau)$}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{abstract}
We study a two-parameter family of affine-invariant Gaussian gradient flows for
the variational problem $\min_{m,C}\KL(\N(m,C)\,\|\,\pi)$. The family is
parameterized by $(\omega,\tau)$: the scalar $\omega>0$ rescales the covariance
dynamics uniformly, while $\tau>-\omega/n$ enters only through a trace-weighting
term and therefore acts solely on the volume (trace) mode of the covariance. A
linearization near stationarity predicts that the trace-mode rate is
$\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=1/[2(\omega+n\tau)]$, so a negative $\tau$ accelerates
volume-dominated transients while leaving the mean and the traceless
shape modes unchanged. We verify these predictions on two targets---an exact
Gaussian and a strongly log-concave non-Gaussian target---using an
exponential-map discretization that preserves positive-definiteness. The
measured time-to-tolerance ratios match the predicted factors ($\approx 2\times$
acceleration under $\tau_-=-\omega/2n$, $\approx 3/2$ slowdown under
$\tau_+=+\omega/2n$) for volume-dominated initializations, and the realized
speedup tracks the initial trace-dominance of the perturbation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Setup}

Let the variational family be the non-degenerate Gaussians $q_{m,C}=\N(m,C)$ with
$m\in\R^{n}$ and $C\in\SPD(n)$, and let the target be
$\pi(\theta)\propto\exp(-V(\theta))$ with $V$ smooth. The objective is the
reverse Kullback--Leibler divergence
\begin{equation}
F(m,C)=\KL\big(\N(m,C)\,\|\,\pi\big)
      =\E_{\theta\sim\N(m,C)}[V(\theta)]-\tfrac12\log\det C + \text{const}.
\end{equation}
Writing $g(m,C)=\E_{q_{m,C}}[\grad V]$ and $S(m,C)=\E_{q_{m,C}}[\grad^2 V]$, the
first-order stationarity conditions are $g(m_\star,C_\star)=0$ and
$C_\star^{-1}=S(m_\star,C_\star)$.

\subsection{Classification of affine-invariant metrics}

A Riemannian metric on the Gaussian manifold
$\Aspace=\R^{n}\times\SPD(n)$ is \emph{affine-invariant} if it is preserved by the
pushforward of every invertible affine map $\theta\mapsto A\theta+b$.

\begin{theorem}[Classification]\label{thm:metric}
Every affine-invariant Riemannian metric on $\Aspace$ has the form
\begin{equation}
g_a\big((u,X),(v,Y)\big)
  = u^\top C^{-1} v
  + \omega\,\Tr(C^{-1}XC^{-1}Y)
  + \tau\,\Tr(C^{-1}X)\,\Tr(C^{-1}Y),
\end{equation}
with $a=(m,C)$, $\omega>0$, and $\tau>-\omega/n$. There is no mean--covariance
coupling.
\end{theorem}

The admissibility condition $\omega+n\tau>0$ is what keeps the induced flow
well-posed; it follows from positive-definiteness of the metric on the trace
mode.

\subsection{The $(\omega,\tau)$ family of flows}

Taking $F$ as the energy and the metric of Theorem~\ref{thm:metric} as the
geometry, the gradient flow is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:flow}
\dot m_t=-C_t\,g(m_t,C_t),\qquad
\dot C_t=\frac{1}{2\omega}\,C_t^{1/2}\big(-B_t+\alpha_t I\big)C_t^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where $B_t=C_t^{1/2}S(m_t,C_t)C_t^{1/2}$ is the Hessian expectation in the
whitened coordinates of $C_t$ and
\begin{equation}
\alpha_t=\frac{\omega+\tau\,\Tr(B_t)}{\omega+n\tau}
\end{equation}
is a scalar trace-weighting term. The parameter $\omega$ rescales the entire
covariance equation; $\tau$ enters \emph{only} through the scalar $\alpha_t$, and
$\alpha_t$ multiplies the identity, so $\tau$ acts only on the trace of the
covariance residual.

\subsection{Exponential-map discretization}

With step $\Delta t$ the implemented update is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:expmap}
m_{k+1}=m_k-\Delta t\,C_k\,g(m_k,C_k),\qquad
C_{k+1}=C_k^{1/2}\exp\!\Big(\tfrac{\Delta t}{2\omega}\big(-B_k+\alpha_k I\big)\Big)C_k^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Because $-B_k+\alpha_k I$ is symmetric, its matrix exponential is symmetric
positive definite, and $C_{k+1}$ is a congruence transform of an SPD matrix;
hence $C_{k+1}\in\SPD(n)$ at every step, with no projection. This is the discrete
exponential map on the SPD manifold.

\section{Trace-mode linearization}\label{sec:linearization}

The effect of $\tau$ is made precise by linearizing near stationarity. Consider
an isotropic perturbation of the whitened Hessian, $B=(1+\varepsilon)I$, so that
$\Tr(B)=n(1+\varepsilon)$ and
\begin{equation}
\alpha=\frac{\omega+\tau n(1+\varepsilon)}{\omega+n\tau}
       =1+\frac{n\tau}{\omega+n\tau}\,\varepsilon,
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
-B+\alpha I=-\frac{\omega}{\omega+n\tau}\,\varepsilon\,I.
\end{equation}
Substituting into \eqref{eq:flow} near $C=I$ gives a linear decay
$\dot\varepsilon=-\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}\,\varepsilon$ for the trace mode with rate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kappatr}
\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=\frac{1}{2(\omega+n\tau)}.
\end{equation}
Three consequences follow for the volume/trace mode, all independent of the
mean and shape modes:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item $\tau=0$: rate $\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=1/(2\omega)$;
  \item $\tau_-=-\omega/2n$: $\omega+n\tau=\omega/2$, so $\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=1/\omega$ --- the rate doubles, the convergence time halves;
  \item $\tau_+=+\omega/2n$: $\omega+n\tau=3\omega/2$, so $\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=1/(3\omega)$ --- the time scale grows by $3/2$.
\end{itemize}
The mean equation in \eqref{eq:flow} does not contain $\tau$, and a traceless
(shape) residual has $\Tr=0$, on which $\alpha$ has no leading-order effect. Thus
$\tau$ can accelerate \emph{only} a trace-dominated transient.

To quantify how trace-dominated a residual is, decompose the covariance residual
$R_{\mathrm{cov}}$ into trace and traceless parts and define the
\emph{trace-dominance ratio}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chi}
\chi=\frac{\big(\Tr R_{\mathrm{cov}}\big)^2}{n\,\|R_{\mathrm{cov}}\|_F^2}\in[0,1],
\end{equation}
with $\chi\approx1$ a purely volume residual and $\chi\approx0$ a purely shape
residual. By \eqref{eq:kappatr} the realized $\tau$-speedup should be largest
when $\chi_0\approx1$.

\section{Experiments}

Two targets are used. The \textbf{Gaussian} target $V(\theta)=\tfrac12\|\theta\|^2$,
$\pi=\N(0,I)$, has exact expectations $g(m,C)=m$, $S(m,C)=I$, equilibrium
$(m_\star,C_\star)=(0,I)$, and admits an exact diagonal covariance update in the
eigenbasis of $C$. The \textbf{log-concave} target
\begin{equation}
V_\rho(x)=\tfrac12\|x\|^2+\frac{\rho}{m}\sum_{\ell=1}^{m}\log\cosh(a_\ell^\top x),
\qquad
I\preceq\grad^2 V_\rho\preceq(1+\rho)I,
\end{equation}
with $m=4n$ fixed unit directions $a_\ell$, is $1$-strongly convex and
$(1+\rho)$-smooth. Its expectations are estimated with a fixed Monte Carlo sample
set (common random numbers across all $(\omega,\tau,\text{init})$ runs), and the
reference optimum $a_\star=(m_\star,C_\star)$ is computed by L-BFGS-B on a
fixed-sample estimate of $F$ over a Cholesky factor.

Five initializations isolate the convergence modes: \emph{mean} (mean
displaced, $C_0=C_\star$), \emph{vol-high}/\emph{vol-low} (isotropic
$C_0=4C_\star$ / $0.25\,C_\star$, $\chi_0\approx1$), \emph{shape} (traceless,
$\det C_0=\det C_\star$, $\chi_0\approx0$), and \emph{mixed} (all modes). For
each $\omega$ the three values $\tau\in\{-\omega/2n,0,+\omega/2n\}$ are run. The
grid metadata is given in Table~\ref{tab:ot-meta}.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{Time scale set by $\omega$.}
Figure~\ref{fig:ot-ttt} shows the time-to-tolerance at $\tau=0$ as a function of
$\omega$ and initialization, for both targets. The mean mode is essentially flat
in $\omega$ (its equation does not involve $\omega$ or $\tau$), whereas the
covariance-dominated modes slow monotonically as $\omega$ grows, consistent with
the $\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}\propto1/\omega$ scaling of \eqref{eq:kappatr}.

\paragraph{$\tau$ accelerates only the volume mode.}
Figure~\ref{fig:ot-speedup} reports the time-to-tolerance ratio $T(\tau)/T(0)$.
For both targets and uniformly in $\omega$, the two volume initializations
accelerate by a factor close to $2$ under $\tau_-$ (ratios $0.47$--$0.51$) and
slow by a factor close to $3/2$ under $\tau_+$ (ratios $1.47$--$1.51$), matching
the predicted trace-mode rates of Section~\ref{sec:linearization}. The mean and
shape modes stay within a few percent of $1$, confirming that $\tau$ leaves them
unaffected. Table~\ref{tab:ot-speedup} lists the ratios at $\omega=0.5$.

\paragraph{The speedup tracks trace dominance.}
Figure~\ref{fig:ot-chi} plots the realized $\tau_-$ speedup against the initial
trace-dominance $\chi_0$ of \eqref{eq:chi} for the log-concave target. The
near-$2\times$ acceleration ($T(\tau_-)/T(0)\approx0.5$) is realized only for
$\chi_0\approx1$; as $\chi_0\to0$ the ratio returns to $1$. This is the
mechanism behind the mode selectivity: $\tau$ rescales the trace channel, so its
benefit is proportional to how much of the limiting error lives in that channel.

\section{Discussion}

Within the affine-invariant family, no single $(\omega,\tau)$ is uniformly
optimal; the parameters trade off cleanly separated channels.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item $\omega$ sets the covariance time scale uniformly. Smaller $\omega$
  accelerates every covariance mode but does not affect the mean; the mean
  channel is governed by its own (parameter-free) equation.
  \item $\tau$ acts only on the volume mode, with rate
  $\kappa_{\mathrm{tr}}=1/[2(\omega+n\tau)]$. A negative $\tau$ (subject to
  $\omega+n\tau>0$) accelerates volume-dominated transients---up to the
  $2\times$ realized here at $\tau_-=-\omega/2n$---and is otherwise neutral.
\end{itemize}
The practical recommendation is therefore conditional on the dominant error
mode: when the limiting error is volume/trace dominated ($\chi_0$ near $1$),
choosing $\tau<0$ as negative as admissibility allows shortens the convergence
time; when the limiting error is shape- or mean-dominated, $\tau$ should be left
at $0$ and only $\omega$ tuned.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The experiments cover $n\le 10$ (Gaussian) and $n=5$ (log-concave) with the
grids of Table~\ref{tab:ot-meta}. The log-concave expectations are Monte Carlo
estimates with a fixed sample set; the normalized objective gap has a numerical
floor near $10^{-4}$ for that run, so the speedup ratios are read at the
$10^{-2}$ tolerance, which is well above the floor. The reported factors are
empirical time-to-tolerance ratios consistent with the linearized rates; they
are not a global convergence proof.
